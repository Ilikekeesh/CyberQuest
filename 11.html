Cybersecurity Lesson: The CIA Triad

Introduction

In today’s digital world, protecting information is more important than ever. From personal accounts to corporate data, cybersecurity helps safeguard sensitive information from unauthorized access, theft, or damage. Understanding cybersecurity is essential for anyone using technology.

Why Cybersecurity Matters

  • Protects Personal Data: Keeps passwords, banking information, and files safe.
  • Prevents Financial Loss: Cyber attacks can cost individuals and organizations millions.
  • Maintains Trust: Companies must protect customer data to maintain credibility.
  • Supports National Security: Securing critical infrastructure prevents threats to safety.

The CIA Triad

The CIA Triad is a foundational model in cybersecurity, representing Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability. Click each section to learn more:

Ensuring information is accessible only to authorized users. Example: Encrypting emails or using strong passwords.

Ensuring information is accurate and unaltered. Example: Using checksums or version control to prevent unauthorized changes.

Ensuring information and systems are accessible when needed. Example: Using backups, redundant servers, and disaster recovery plans.

Real-World Example: Online Banking

  • Confidentiality: Only the account holder can see their balance.
  • Integrity: Transactions are accurate and unchanged.
  • Availability: The website is accessible whenever customers need it.

Conclusion

Cybersecurity is essential for protecting information, maintaining trust, and ensuring systems work as intended. By understanding and applying the CIA Triad, individuals and organizations can build safer digital environments.

Level 1.1: Common Threats


Goals:
  • Learning about common issues and cybersecurity threats
  • Building a mental dictionary of important security terms

In the world of cybersecurity, we deal with a lot of different types of threat actors. In this level, we will learn about the most common types and how they act.

Software Attacks
  • Denial of service (or distributed denial of service)
  • Computer worms, viruses, and other malware
Software Errors
  • Software bugs and human error
  • Applications going offline
  • Cross-site scripts or illegal file server sharing
Sabotage
  • Authorized users penetrating and compromising data
  • Defacement of a organization's website
Human Error
  • Inadvertant data entry error
  • Firewall misconfigurations
Theft
  • Laptops or other data stolen from unlocked rooms
Hardware Failure
  • Hard drive crashes
Utility Interruption
  • Electrical power outage
  • Water damage
Natural Disasters
  • Hurricanes, tornadoes
  • Thunderstorms, flooding
  • Fires, earthquakes
